50,000 SPECIES GO EXTINCT EACH YEAR DUE TO DEFORESTATION

JAGUAR

Scientific name: Panthera onca
Estimated population in wild: 173,000
Decline: Decreasing, in the 1960s/1970s there were approx. 18,000 jaguars were killed each year. In 1996 jaguars were nearly 0 in the United States.
Life span: 12 - 15 years
Location: An estimated 50% of the population are in Brazil. Found in the Amazon rainforest and Pantanal. Tropical wetlands.
IUCN status: Near threatened (2016)
Threatened by: Illegal hunting, deforestation and the loss of wild prey. They often have frequent conflicts with farmers

Fun fact: A study in 2020 found that 25% of Jaguars located in Costa Rica were black due to a dominant allele. They also tend to have increased activity during the full moon, and the spots are still visible from certain angles.

HYACINTH MACAW

Scientific name: Anodorhynchus hyacinthinus
Estimated population in wild: 2,000 - 6,500 hyacinths
Decline: Decreasing
Life span: 50 years
Location: Majority in Pantanal of Brazil, Bolivia, and Northeastern Paraguay
IUCN status: Vulnerable (2016)
Threatened by: Habitat loss and over-collection for the pet trade. An estimated 10,000 birds were taken in the 1980s.
Fun fact: They only eat palm nuts after they pass through a cow.

TIGER

Scientific name: Panthera tigris
Estimated population in wild: Around 4,500
Decline: Suggested 40% increase in population numbers from 3,200 in 2015 to 4,500 in 2022.
Life span: Average maximum of 15 years
Location: Diverse habitats, rain forests, grasslands, savannas, and even swamps. In places like India, Indonesia, and Southwest Asia.
IUCN status: Endangered (2021)
Threatened by: Poached for parts and habitat loss due to humans. 93% of their original land has been lost due to human activity.
Fun fact: Their saliva is antiseptic, they tend to use this on wounds to prevent infection

SPIDER MONKEY

Scientific name: Ateles
Estimated population in wild: Less than 3,000, Brown-headed with an estimation of 250 individuals
Decline: 30% decrease over 45 years
Life span: 27 years in the wild
Location: Tropical rainforests in Central and South America
IUCN status: Most are endangered, and all are threatened Brown-headed Spider Monkey - Endangered (2020)
Threatened by: Destruction of the rainforest and hunters. Forest fragmentation
Fun fact: They do not have thumbs, but very strong tails, lacking hair on the underside for gripping.

TAPIR

Scientific name: Tapiridae
Estimated population in wild: Between 3,000 and 4,500 depending on species
Decline: Occupies only 1.78% of its original environment, 98.2% reduction in its range.
Life span: 25 - 30 years
Location: Central and South America, Malay Tapirs are the only found in Asia
IUCN status: Classified as Vulnerable or endangered. Malay Tapir - Endangered (2014)
Threatened by: Deforestation, hunting for hides and meat alongside settlement, and competition with livestock.
Fun fact: They are often called the “Gardeners of the forest” as they play an important role in the dispersing of seeds. Due to their size, they can move many seeds.

ORANGUTAN

Scientific name: Pongo pygmaeus
Estimated population in wild: 230,000. However Bornean orangutan with 104,700 and about 7,500 Sumatran orangutans.
Decline: Sharp decline in the numbers of orangutans due to human activity, and it is likely without much change, orangutans may go extinct in 20 years.
Life span: 35 - 45 years
Location: Mainly Southeast Asian islands or Borneo and Sumatra. 80% of orangutans are located in Indonesia.
IUCN status: All critically endangered. Sumatran Orangutan - Critically Endangered (2017)
Threatened by: Hunting and conversion of forest for agricultural land, often being done illegally. Mothers are often killed for their infants for illegal pets, which are of high value.

Fun fact: There are a total of 3 species of Orangutan. Sumatran, Bornean and Tapanuli. They like comfort and will take 10 minutes to build nests to sleep in, using branches as a mattress while weaving them together. During wet nights they may make a roof, they make a nest every day and this is often used by humans to predict populations.

EASTERN GORILLA

Scientific name: Gorilla beringei graueri
Estimated population in wild: 2,600 mature individuals
Decline: An estimated decline of more than 50% since the 1990s, when the population was 17,000
Life span: 35 - 50 years
Location: Lowland tropical rainforests in the Eastern Democratic Republic of the Congo
IUCN status: Eastern Gorilla - Critically Endangered (2018), Western Gorilla - Critically Endangered (2016)
Threatened by: Poaching, habitat loss, and diseases. Gorillas are regarded as bushmeat and are hunted for bush meat, there is also weak law enforcement surrounding this.

Fun fact: They are the largest primates in the world.

RING TAILED LEMUR

Scientific name: Lemur catta
Estimated population in wild: 2,220
Decline: An estimated decline of 95% since 2000
Life span: 16 - 19 years
Location: Southwestern Madagascar, in the rainforest
IUCN status: Endangered (2020)
Threatened by: Deforestation and being hunted for bushmeat or pet trade.
Fun fact: Their tails are longer than their bodies, they are crucial seed dispersers and are often called the “creator of the forests”, and they mainly reside in Madagascar.
