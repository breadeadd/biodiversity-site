LOGGERHEAD TURTLE

Scientific name: Caretta caretta
Estimated population in wild: Estimated 50,000
Decline: Global population estimated decline of 47% since long-term monitoring since 10 - 50 years ago.
Life span: 70 - 80 years
Location: Subtropical and temperate regions of the Atlantic, Pacific, and Indian Oceans, and the Mediterranean Sea.
NOAA status: Endangered
Threatened by: Climate change, marine debris, pollution, chemicals, and fisheries bycatch

Fun fact: They are named after their broad and muscular heads that have a blunt jaw that can crush hard-shelled prey.

ORCA

Scientific name: Orcinus orca
Estimated population in wild: Estimated 50,000 globally
Decline: Southern Resident Killer Whale population declined by 25% from the peak population in 1995
Life span: Average of 50 years for females but can be up to 80 - 90
Location: 2,500 of the population located in the North Pacific Ocean
IUCN status: Data deficient
Threatened by: Lack of food due to overfishing, pollution, oil spills, noise pollution from vessels, and entanglement with fishing gear.
Fun fact: Although they are called Killer Whales, they are actually dolphins, and different orca clans speak different languages.

MONK SEAL

Scientific name: Monachini
Estimated population in wild: 1,570 Hawaiian monk seals are estimated to be left
Decline: 60% in the past 50 years. Since 1998, the number of Hawaiian monk seals decreased by 4.1% annually
Life span: Up to 30 years
Location: Northwestern Hawaiian Islands
IUCN status: Hawaiian Monk Seal - Endangered (2015)
Threatened by: Food limitation, human interactions, and entanglement alongside shark predation.

Fun fact: They can grow up to 2.1 metres long and weigh 272 kg. They can stay underwater for 20 minutes and shed their top layer of fur annually. This is called “catastrophic molt.” Hawaiian Monk Seals are often found sleeping in underwater caves or beaches.

DUGONG

Scientific name: Dugong dugon
Estimated population in wild: Around 2,500
Decline: Estimated to be less than the estimated amount, with a 20% decline in the last 90 years
Life span: 70 years
Location: Warm coastal waters, East Africa, Australia, Red Sea, Pacific, and Indian ocean
IUCN status: Vulnerable (2015)
Threatened by: Loss of seagrass, without optimal seagrass, they do not breed properly. Water pollution and industrial activities. They can also get entangled in fishing gear and nets.
Fun fact: They are important to maintain coastal ecosystems and are often called “Sea Cows”

MANATEE

Scientific name: Trichechus
Estimated population in wild: At least 13,000
Decline: In Florida, there was a recorded 6,620 Manatees in early 2017 to 5,733 in 2019
Life span: 65 years
Location: Estimated more than 6,500 in the United States and Puerto Rico
IUCN status: African Manatee - Vulnerable (2015), American Manatee - Vulnerable (2008)
Threatened by: Loss of habitat and collision with boats and ships due to their naturally curious behaviour
Fun fact: Sailors have mistaken the manatee to be mermaids, including Christopher Columbus.

WHALE SHARK

Scientific name: Rhincodon typus
Estimated population in wild: Estimated 119,000–238,000
Decline: Estimated decrease of 50% over 75 years.
Life span: 130 years
Location: Tropical oceans
IUCN status: Endangered (2016)
Green Status Assessment: Largely depleted (2021) 29% Recovery score
Threatened by: Unregulated fisheries due to the high demand for meat, fins, and oil. They are victims of bycatch and accidental capture in fishing gear

Fun fact: Some Whale sharks have teeth on their eyes. Although they have thousands of teeth in their mouths they do not eat, they are filter feeders and instead open up their 1.5-metre-wide mouths to catch small fish, plankton, and more.

SEA LION

Scientific name: Otariinae
Estimated population in wild: Mature individuals - 6,500
Decline: Declining
Life span: 20 - 30 years
Location: All over the world, from Mexico to Australia, however not in the Northern Atlantic Ocean.
IUCN status: Australian Sea Lion - Endangered (2014)
Threatened by: Decrease in fish populations, currents changing due to climate change, bycatch, entanglement, and introduced species bringing diseases
Fun fact: Sea lions can walk and even run on land. They are very skilled hunters and incredibly social creatures.

BLUE WHALE

Scientific name: Balaenoptera musculus
Estimated population in wild: 5,000 - 15,000 mature individuals
Decline: Increasing
Life span: 80 - 90 years
Location: All oceans apart from the Arctic
IUCN status: Endangered (2018)
Threatened by: Habitat loss and toxins. Alongside fishing gear and ship strikes.

Fun fact: The largest animals to have lived on Earth, growing up to 100 feet long and 200 tonnes. Their tongues are the weight of an elephant and their heart is the weight of an automobile.
